Pythagorean Theorum - Documentations

The Pythagorean theorem is a rule about right‑angled triangles: if a triangle has a 90° corner, then
short side2 + other short side2 = long side2

1. What type of triangle are we talking about?


A right-angled triangle is a triangle with one corner that is exactly 90 (like the corner of a piece of paper).

The longest side, opposite the 90° angle, is called the hypotenuse. The other two sides are just the two shorter sides (often called a and b).

2. What does the theorem actually mean?


Take each short side, multiply it by itself (that’s what “squared” means), then add those two results.

You will get the same number as if you square the long side (the hypotenuse):
a2 + b2 = c2

Example: suppose the two short sides are 3 and 4.
32 = 9,
42 = 16, and
9 + 16 = 25.

The long side must satisfy c2 = 25, so c = 5. That's why 3-4-5 is a famous right triangle.

3. How to use it?


If you know the two short sides and want the long side:

Square each short side (a and b).

Add the two answers: a2 + b2.

Take the square root of that sum to get the hypotenuse c.

If you know the hypotenuse and one short side and want the missing short side:

Square the hypotenuse and the known short side.

Subtract: hypotenuse2 - known short side2.

Take the square root of that result to get the missing side.

4. Practice questions


Find the hypotenuse of triangles with base and perpendicular:
  • 3, 4
  • 5, 12
  • 6, 8
